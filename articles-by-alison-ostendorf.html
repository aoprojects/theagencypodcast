How to Spice Up Your Web Design

10 design changes you can make that will add some spice to your website

In order of what's easy to implement... (add checklist visuals to the left of each item)

  1. Mobile responsive design We don't need to say much about this one. It's 2018. We're a mobile economy. Build everything to render well on any device. Just do it.
  2. Add Color should have 2 - 3 base colors and an accent brand color {show visual example of this in color swatch with blue or orange}
  3. Varying font sizes and weights
  4. Hover effects Text that changes color on hover Subtle shapes that appear over photographs in a gallery Items that move left or right, or start animating, when hovered over by the user. Tip: {icon for pointer/tip} Do not use hover-over navigations for touch screen devices. There is no mouse for which hovering over can occur, and any hover-only reveals will not work for mobile devices and any other touch-only devices.
  5. Shapes Not everything has to make sense. Shapes help break up the visual space, and if used right, they help focus on the more important content areas. Shapes are also evocative visually and help tell your story similarly to the way icons do. {Example: }
  6. Icons - Text engages search enginges. Visuals engage humans. aka "Flaticons" or just "icons" - adding illustrations of what you're writing about helps users skim your page. It is a visual demosnstration of the writing on your page. In fact, if you could demonstrate what you're saying with just images and a little accompanying text (like a cartoon), it would probably be most effective way to keep a user engaged.
  7. Text Animations Reveals, parallax, etc. of elements on your page. Stuff that makes your page feel like a digital verion of a living thing. {example} use animation.css or another pre-made stylesheet. {link}
  8. Pictures People are coming to your site with the goal of picturing who your company is. So give them an actual picture of whatever physical represnetation of your brand you can! product photography photos of your physcial store photos of the members of your team stock photos (if they make sense) Example/tip: you can do this in a carousel, a grid, a full screen image, etc.
  9. Video If you have something that is actually moving and can entertain a viewer, put it front and center. Video is an easy answer to that. the Users want an excuse to watch things while still "getting work done". Or... they just want an excuse to watch things. Moving things will almost always grab attentoin mroe than static things. Warning: To be used in moderation. You're not designing a digital circus, and if you were, you'd still only have a few things moving around at a time. Tips: If it's a full video, don't annoy your users by auto-payign it upon load of the page. If a viewer does click on the video, allow it to take front and center (which usually means to put it in a modal pop up and play after the first click on the video thumbnail).
  10. Translucent vs Opaque items take some of the above items and add some translucence and overlap to them. Allow them to play with each other. {example jetblue icon of new york skyline flying}
tip: mix these elemtns with each other. Example: a restaurant business home page could display images of their best food, on top of which a slow reveal of colorful text and shapes appears. As the user scrolls don; the text, the shape, and the image all scroll at differnet speeds (parallax).
